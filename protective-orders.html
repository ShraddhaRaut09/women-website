Protective Orders

Learn about protective orders, how they work, and how they can help protect you from abuse and harassment.

What Is a Protective Order?

A protective order (also known as a restraining order) is a legal order issued by a court to prevent an abuser from contacting or coming near the victim. It is designed to provide immediate protection to the victim of abuse or harassment.

Types of Protective Orders

There are several types of protective orders, each offering different levels of protection. The most common types include:

  • Emergency Protective Order (EPO): A short-term order issued by a judge or law enforcement officer to provide immediate protection, typically lasting a few days.
  • Temporary Protective Order (TPO): A temporary order issued by a court that provides protection for a longer period, typically a few weeks, until a final order is made.
  • Permanent Protective Order: A long-term order that can last for months or years, providing sustained protection for the victim.

How to Obtain a Protective Order

If you are experiencing abuse or harassment, you may be eligible to request a protective order. Here's how you can apply:

  • File a Petition: Visit your local courthouse or family court to file a petition for a protective order. Some jurisdictions also allow online applications.
  • Provide Evidence: You may need to provide evidence of abuse, such as photographs, police reports, or witness statements.
  • Attend a Hearing: A court hearing may be scheduled where both you and the alleged abuser can present your case. In some cases, a temporary order may be issued immediately.

What Protective Orders Do

Once granted, a protective order can provide various forms of protection, such as:

  • No Contact: The abuser is ordered to stop all contact, including phone calls, emails, and in-person communication.
  • Stay Away: The abuser is ordered to stay a certain distance away from the victim’s home, workplace, and other frequent locations.
  • Child Custody and Visitation: A protective order may also modify child custody and visitation arrangements if necessary for the child’s safety.
  • Return of Property: The abuser may be ordered to return property, such as personal items or documents, to the victim.

Resources for Legal Support

If you are seeking help with filing for a protective order, or need assistance understanding your legal options, here are some organizations that can help:

  • National Domestic Violence Hotline - 1-800-799-7233
  • Women's Law - Offers information and resources on protective orders and legal support.
  • ACLU - Advocates for the protection of civil rights, including from domestic violence.
  • LegalMatch - Helps connect individuals with lawyers who specialize in family law and protective orders.
